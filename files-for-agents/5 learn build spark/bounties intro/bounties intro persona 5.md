## Two Good Case Study (summarized)

“She didn’t need saving. She needed a shift.” For women who’ve survived trauma, the first loss is often belief in their own worth. Handouts can lock in the “recipient” role. Two Good flips that: a real kitchen business (orders, customers, wages) that changes lives. Dignity starts when she picks up the tools.

Domestic and family violence is the main driver of homelessness for women and children; in 2023–24, 39% of people seeking homelessness services were fleeing violence. The gap Rob targets: between surviving and building—emergency services get people out but don’t get them forward. Safety is necessary; agency makes it stick. Two Good focuses on employment as that bridge.

Theory of change: 
(1) First thing lost: not housing or income first—self-worth. (2) From recipient to contributor: Work Work = 4 months in a real commercial kitchen (catering, real customers). She’s no longer “being helped”; she’s contributing. (3) Layers: Dignity (trust, tasks that matter) → connection (peers, mentors) → skill (orders, products as proof) → pathway out (employment with partners). (4) High standards: Real pressure and excellence = no patronage; the bar is respect. “The job is the intervention”: paid work cuts financial stress and repairs identity at once; agency is the antidote to trauma, career the sustainable form of safety.

Business model: 
Women are the business, not a cost centre; funding comes from customers, not grants. Began as buy-one-give-one; losses on training led to making training the product (Work Work). Revenue and impact scale together: more orders → more shifts → more wages and experience. FY2025: 47 women, 22,629 hours, $726k+ wages; one big corporate contract ≈ 120 hours of paid work.

Social procurement: 
Durable revenue = contracts, not one-off spikes. B2B social procurement (e.g. Charter Hall >$1M in FY24, washroom products, ocean-plastic dispensers) turns ESG spend into predictable payroll. Multi-year deals give the trauma-informed program the stability it needs.

Real demand: 
Corporate clients (e.g. Google, Atlassian) pay for quality; late delivery and sloppy batches matter. Excellence is the strategy; impact is the bonus. Paul Ramsay Foundation evaluations show meaningful gains in self-worth, confidence, and hope. Employment Partner Framework: graduates move into partners (Charter Hall, QBE, GPT); 19 women placed in ongoing jobs. The end outcome is career, not just a meal.
